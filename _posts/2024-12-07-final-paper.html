Localized Weighted Logistic Regression for Spatial Prediction of Child Vaccine Uptake in Narok County, Kenya

Jimmy Bach

Introduction

In low and middle-income countries (LMICs), public health resources such as vaccines are often very difficult for citizens to access. This is due to a multitude of reasons, including diminished supply, lack of knowledge, and fewer health professionals. However, specifically in rural areas of LMICs, one of the largest reasons is geographic inaccessibility. A lack of hospitals/health facilities coupled with less modes of transportation make spatial location one of the most important factors in determining health outcomes in these regions.

Within Narok County, Kenya, however, spatial location becomes slightly more complex. Many people get a large sense of identity from their tribe or community. The most preeminent example of this is the Maasai, the largest tribe in Narok County. The Maasai are a nomadic pastoral tribe, meaning that their location changes very often as they seasonally move to find water and pasture. Since they have such a strong sense of community with those within their tribe, the Maasai tend to be very similar in socioeconomic status and views as their neighbors. Thus, location data holds importance in a separate way as well within these communities. Because it can be used as a strong indicator of tribal homophily in the Maasai and other groups in Narok County, it can be easily used to predict whether individuals within a tribe will receive the same health outcomes as others in that location.

Since we are likely to see clustering of individuals occur, the coordinates of an individual are unlikely to be normally distributed in these rural areas. In addition, due to the nomadic lifestyle of tribes such as the Maasai, it becomes difficult to establish a global relationship between location and health outcomes, as they often move around in unpredictable ways. However, because of the homophily and tribal identity between members of the Maasai, local relationships are very strong predictors in many circumstances. This makes localized weighted regression (or LWR) a useful model to consider within the context of Narok County childhood vaccination.

What is Localized Weighted Regression?

What is Localized Weighted Regression? In essence, LWR calculates the expected value of the conditional expectation of an output y given that a certain input X is equal to a specific x value (Eq. 1). In the context of this paper, the X values are the latitude and longitude coordinates of a child's village location. Although LWR does in many cases utilize linear regression and its regularized offsets (ex: Ridge) at a local level, it contrasts from these forms of regression and is considered non-parametric because we cannot make any assumptions about the relationship between input and output at a global scale within the dataset.

(1)E[y|x=x]=f(x),the conditional expectation formula fundamental to Loess

Besides an input and output, LWR also takes 2 additional things into account- a kernel function and a tau value.

Definitions

Kernel Function: A Kernel function is a function bound by specific values and 0 otherwise. Typically, kernel functions have a local maximum in the middle of the support window and taper towards 0 on the edges. One example is a Gaussian (normal) distribution bounded on each side (Eq.2).

Tau: The Tau value, otherwise known as the bandwidth, helps set a restriction on what the weight values for your inputs can be.

In conjunction with each other, a kernel and bandwidth help create a matrix of weights. These weights are calculated by taking the distance between one set of points and another, point by point. In practice (and especially in machine learning), this is often a training set and test set of your inputs. The distance values, after being divided by the tau (hence restricting the bandwidth), are inputted into the kernel. The local maximum of a kernel function represents points 0 units apart (i.e. the same point) and will produce higher weights, with larger distances (less similar points) tapering off in weight values as they're either on the edge or out of the bounds of the kernel (which reduces the weight to 0).

(2)K(x):=e−∥x∥22τ,the Gaussian kernel

The weights reinforce the idea that we are looking for local, not global, relationships. A training point that has input values similar to a testing point's input values is likely to have a strong ability to predict the test point's output. This is the case in basic regressions such as linear regression as well, but the key difference there is that if our X and y present a non-linear relationship, linear regression fails because it attempts to treat every point as equally important to establishing a prediction for a specific point. Instead, we can utilize linear regression within LWR by fitting our weighted data to a linear model. This approach leads to a set of different linear relationships throughout the data that can approximate an overall non-linear relationship, rather than simply one "line of best fit". It's also for this reason that we begin to see a smoothing effect in LWR models.

LWR models can also be used for classification, not just regression. One of the most common classification machine learning techniques is logistic regression. Where for regression techniques the cost function that is being minimized is often mean squared error, localized weighted logistic regression (LWLR) looks to minimize the following function (in the case of 2 categories):

(3)loss=−y∗ln(p)−(1−y)∗ln(1−p)

Where (p,1-p) are the predicted probabilities for classes (y,1-y), which will either have a value of 0 or 1. This function is better known as binary cross-entropy. To get the probabilities, logistic regression utilizes the sigmoid function, shown below:

(4)p(x)=11+e−(β0+β1x)

Data/Methodology

Data Used

The data used for this paper was collected via I and other students with the Ignite Global Health Research Lab, located within the College of William and Mary's Global Research Institute. Data was collected from and with the consent of Community Health Partners (CHP), the largest community health clinic group in Narok County.

Subsetting the Data

Data with null values were dropped due to concerns with reliability of these observations in the recording process. From there, since LWR is a heavily computationally expensive algorithm, data was subsetted to include only the past two years, a step which also helped to ensure the observations being looked at were more reflective of the current state of vaccination among Narok children in light of the recovery from health supply shocks caused by the COVID-19 pandemic both locally and globally. Finally, data was dropped which displayed inconsistencies in the first visit date and date of birth, another step to ensure only reliable observations were being used. These steps resulted in a reduction in the observations from n=9,718 to n=2,278.

Feature Selection

Since data in developing countries such as Kenya can be very difficult to come by, developing a model which can make successful predictions in low feature space is imperative. As outlined in the introduction, location is a powerful predictor of health outcomes such as childhood vaccination due to the strong sense of community within tribes. Thus, the only features used for modeling purposes were the coordinate data (latitude and longitude) of peoples' home villages.

Target Variables

There are 15* different target variables in the data, one for each type of vaccine tracked in the dataset. The vaccines in the data include: BCG, Polio, DPT1-3, OPV1-3, PCV1-3, Rotavirus1-2, Vitamin A, and Measles and Rubella. Doses were tracked by the dates the children received the vaccine, with children who didn't receive the vaccine left as a null value. These variables were transformed into binary variables by changing the null values to zeroes (indicating no vaccination) and the dates to ones (indicating vaccination).

Models Used

Since the target variables have been specified as binary variables, classification models were used rather than regressors. Thus, LWLR was performed rather than LWR. To compare the success of these models, XGBoost classification was also performed. XGBoost is one of the most popular methods of classification due to the success, scalability, and efficiency of its computations. It involves gradient boosting of a decision tree and has many built in features such as regularization and cross-validation. Finally, as a "control" model, binary logistic regression was also performed on each vaccine. With 15 total vaccines and 3 different types of models, a total of 45 different models were created.

Methodology

Dealing with Imbalanced Data

There are some vaccines which nearly every child in the dataset received, while other vaccines were barely received by anyone. Because of this, for every target being looked at, some sort of imbalance between the two classes (receiving and not receiving the vaccine) exists. Without accounting for this imbalance, models tend to overpredict the majority class, leading to very high recall of one class and low recall of the other. In order to improve the overall performance of the model in consideration of both the majority and minority classes, Synthetic Minority Oversampling Technique (or SMOTE) was performed. SMOTE oversamples the minority class to create artificial ("Synthetic") observations using the nearest neighbors to minority points.

Hyperparameter Tuning

Different approaches were taken to hyperparameter tuning for each of the three model types.

LWLR

Due to the computational intensity and time inefficiency of Localized Weighted Logistic Regression along with the fact that there are over 2000 observations in the dataset, a robust approach to tuning the hyperparameters of these models was not appropriate. Instead, a grid search of four different kernel functions and six different tau values was performed. The functions and values include the following:

XGBoost

In contrast to LWLR, since XGBoost is able to perform calculations far quicker, testing more hyperparameters was feasible. Thus, instead of a simple grid search of very few values, Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO) of three XGBoost hyperparameters was performed. PSO considers a group of "particles" (points within bounds for multiple hyperparameters) and iteratively updates the position of the particles within the grid by considering the best position of a particle and its neighbors. The formulas behind updating the particle position are as follows:

(11)vi(t+1)=w⋅vi(t)+c1⋅χ⋅(pibest(t)−pi(t))+c2⋅χ⋅(pgbest(t)−pi(t))

where

(12)χ∼Unif(0,1)

and c1 and c2 are constants. The particle positions get updated:

(13)pi(t+1)=pi(t)+vi(t)

In order to avoid adding unnecessary complexity to the process, w, c1, and c2, which are all hyperparameters in the PSO algorithm, are kept constant at 0.4, 0.5, and 0.8 respectively. The three hyperparameters of XGBoost which were optimized were the learning rate (eta) bounded between [0.01,2], the minimum split loss to further partition the XGBoost decision tree (gamma) bounded between [0,5], and maximum depth of the tree bounded between [1,10]. 100 iterations of PSO were performed with three total particles being used to optimize the position.

Logistic Regression

Since the logistic regression models are ultimately meant to serve as a control model, hyperparameter tuning was not performed. Instead, for these models, the Sci-Kit Learn library in python was used, and the baseline hyperparameters in its LogisticRegression() class were selected.

Evaluation/Cross Validation

For each type of model, 10-Fold cross validation was performed. To avoid data leakage involving synthetic observations created from SMOTE, the testing folds only involved real observations, and synthetic data was only used to help train the model. Models were tested using the F1-score, or the harmonic mean of precision and recall. F1-score was the selected metric because models that produce higher values typically have good diagnostic ability for both classes, which is important considering the class imbalances that appear in the dataset. Hyperparameter combinations with the highest F1-score were selected for each LWLR and XGBoost model.

Comparing the Models

Across 10 of the 15 vaccines, LWLR performed the best of the three types of models looked at by F1-score. All 10 were sequential vaccines, with the only sequential vaccines that didn't perform best under LWLR being OPV3 and ROTA2 (best under XGBoost). However, on vaccines that were meant to be given at birth (BCG and polio), XGBoost and LWLR performed almost exactly the same, with F-1 scores within 0.002 of each other. Additionally, XGBoost also outperformed LWLR on the last two vaccines observed within the vaccine calendar, Vitamin A and MR. Logistic Regression did not perform the best for any of the vaccines.

Conclusion/Limitations

In comparing the predictive ability of LWLR, XGBoost, and Logistic Regression, LWLR appears to show the most predictive ability with XGBoost also appearing to be an effective modeling choice of modeling childhood vaccine uptake in Narok, Kenya. This is an important step in modeling community-level health data. In a field like global health where data can be sparse at this level, finding a more effective model in low feature space is of paramount concern. Using a variable such as location of someone's home village, which is widely available in many cases, makes this a simple process to replicate. In addition, in a population where community and tribal identity is very important, village locations are a good proxy for these populations, which are likely to live together and move about together. LWLR is able to best model this process due to its ability to set the width of the kernel such that the only points within the kernel are those of people living in the same or very closeby villages.

There are very important limitations to this study. First, LWLR does not generalize well to larger amounts of features and becomes an inefficient to run model. If more features are included in the calculations and available in the data, using speedier algorithms such as XGBoost and Logistic Regression serve a benefit of easily computing results in comparison to LWLR. This same issue arises with an increase in the number of observations within the dataset. It is also important to acknowledge that tribes across the world are not monolithic in nature, and thus may display different patterns relating to group identity and migratory patterns than those who are in Narok.
